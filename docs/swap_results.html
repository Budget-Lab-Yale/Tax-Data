TLDR

We added an amount term to the swap solver's objective. The result is that Step B (the per-cell rescale that was distorting top shares) is essentially eliminated — the typical cell now needs no rescale at all (median factor 1.000 vs 0.840 before). Top-1% NW share is +0.35pp from SCF (was +1.35pp). Counts are slightly worse on the headline ±5% metric (43.8% vs 47.9%), an acceptable trade for the much better amounts and top shares.

What problem are we solving?

We have ~221k PUF tax units. Each one needs to get a wealth profile (23 dollar variables: equities, bonds, primary home, debts, etc.). We do this by donor matching: pick an SCF household with similar income and demographics, copy its wealth profile.

The matching happens via a random forest. For each PUF tax unit, the forest gives a "leaf" of ~30–50 SCF donors that look like it on income/demographics. Step A picks one donor per record from that leaf.

We split the population into 16 cells (8 income buckets × 2 age groups) and 6 wealth categories (nw, equities, bonds, homes, other, debt), giving 96 buckets. For each bucket, SCF gives us:

  • count target — how many households (weighted) hold a positive amount of this category in this cell.
  • amount target — the total dollars (weighted) held in this category in this cell.

Our donor picks should reproduce both per cell.

What was wrong before this experiment

The swap solver only optimized counts. After it finished, the per-cell amounts could be 25–35% over SCF on aggregate — because the leaf donors with the right positivity pattern aren't necessarily the right magnitude.

We had a Step B that fixed the amounts by multiplying every record's wealth in (cell × category) by a corrective factor (typical factor ≈ 0.84). This works in the sense of "the aggregate matches SCF" but it has two costs you flagged:

  • It produces synthetic dollar values that no real SCF donor had.
  • It distorts the within-cell wealth distribution, which inflates measured top shares.

What this experiment changed

We added an amount term to the swap solver's objective. Now each proposed swap is evaluated against:

  • how it changes the counts across all 6 categories (as before)
  • plus how it changes the dollar amounts across the 5 amount-tracked categories (equities, bonds, homes, other, debt — nw is excluded because it's a sum of the others)

The amount_weight knob controls how much the amount term weighs vs. the count term. We tested 0 (count-only, the old behavior), 0.5, 1, and 2. amount_weight = 0.5 turned out to be the sweet spot.

What still doesn't work

  • Top-tail seniors: the cells pct99.9to100/senior, pct80to90/senior, etc. are still off in both counts and amounts. The leaf donor pool just doesn't contain enough of the right-shaped donors. This is a thin-sample problem on the SCF side — only ~570 senior rows in the top 0.1% income cell. Bigger leaves (mns=50 already used here) only partially help.
  • "Other" category: lumps 9 distinct wealth components into one aggregate. The solver has only one knob for an aggregate determined by 9 underlying values. The solver hits the aggregate amount target, but within-aggregate composition can be wrong. To fix this cleanly we would need to split "other" into a few sub-categories.
  • NW pre-rescale gap: nw isn't directly targeted (it's a derived linear combination of the other 5). It still drifts, indirectly, from the residual amount errors in the others. After Step B's now-tiny rescale, final nw amounts are essentially correct.

Recommendation

Adopt the joint solver with amount_weight = 0.5 as the new Step A. This gives:

  • Count match within ±5% of SCF in 44% of buckets (was 48% under count-only — a 4pp drop, acceptable).
  • Amount match within ±5% of SCF in 62% of buckets (was 12% — five-fold improvement).
  • Step B median rescale factor 1.000 (was 0.840) — Step B is essentially a sanity check, not a calibrator.
  • Top-1 NW share gap +0.35pp (was +1.35pp) — well inside the original ≤+1.5pp target.
  • Production output is real donor rows with negligible corrective scaling. The "ugly" data manipulation you were worried about is essentially gone.
